CRI, short for the Global Climate Risk Index, is a figure developed by Germanwatch with the goal of analyzing the quantitative impacts of extreme weather events in terms of the cost of both human and financial losses. A country's ranking, based on their number of fatalities, fatalities per 100,000 inhabitants, losses per million US$, and losses per unit GDP, all resultant from these extreme weather events.

Predictably, most of these countries are either in warm, coastal or island regions who are frequented by tropical storms, or regions subject to extreme temperatures which may causes droughts and famines. But under the surface, there is more at play. The disparities, and where much of the inequality comes from in regard to who is most in danger of climate change resultant events, is in how and what those losses are. Poorer, developing countries which are in danger of climate disasters are at much greater risk because they lack the infrastructure to prepare for and recover from these disasters compared to richer countries. If we take a look at Canada, for instance, it has a CRI score of 87.33. It ranked 15th in the world in average loss in millions of US$ from 2000-2019, but 83rd in average loss per unit GDP in that same time period. Poorer countries do not have this same luxury. These are the 10 countries that Germanwatch determined to be the most at risk for frequent, or rare but catastrophic weather events that result from climate change along with their CRI score, and qualitative categories.

For many of these countries, damages from climate disasters accounts for a significant part of their total GDP. Across these ten, the average ranking for loss per until GDP was 21st. The positive feedback loop of loss, incomplete recovery, and further loss means that many of the developing and poor countries more affected by climate change will continue to sink lower in poverty. A government which is financially unable to support infrastructure to protect its citizens, and unable to rebuilt when catastrophe strikes, will mean that loss of life, personal hardship, and existential threats will become more and more widespread.

On December 12th 2015, 195 countries at COP 21 in Paris came together to adopt the Paris Agreement, a legally binding international treaty on climate change. The goal of the Paris aAgreement is to limit global warming to below 2, preferably 1.5 degrees Celsius compared to preindustrial levels. The Paris Agreement works on a 5-year cycle of climate action carried out by each country. By 2020, the end of the first cycle, countries submit their climate action plans known as nationally determined contributions (NDCs). NDC signifies actions they will take to reduce their Greenhouse Gas emissions in order to reach the Paris Agreeement Goals.
